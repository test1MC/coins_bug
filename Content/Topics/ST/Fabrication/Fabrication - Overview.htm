Fabrication - Overview

Fabrication is to support low volume, high effort making of items, where you want to be able to capture costs from Payroll, and ; for example, bespoke pieces made in a metal shop. These may be one-off items made for a or customer, or may be made to add to your .

Fabrications are different from assemblies, which are more suited for higher volume, lower effort items for which you do not need to capture costs, but can use a standard value; for example, assembly of roof trusses.

Fabricating for

A site orders some bespoke items and you want to charge them actual cost. Payroll, and costs debit the fabrication job card – hitting the WIP account – as the items are fabricated. Once all costs have landed the job card is posted.

Fabricating for Customer

You want to charge a customer a fixed price. The fixed price value creates a zero job card cost with recharge equal to the fixed price. The job card costs from Payroll, and have zero recharge.

For cash flow you may wish to cost to the customer before all costs have hit the job card – there can be quite a delay if costs are involved. The options are to post the job card without closing, or post the job card and allow late costs in configuration.

Posting the job card moves Costs from WIP to workshop costs as in “Fabricating for “, and creates invoice which will cost to the customer and give revenue to the job card.

Fabricating

The job card should be set to recharge to the workshop’s overhead . You can do this at cost or use quantity x unit cost of the item being made for the debit to control. When using a unit cost the difference will be workshop revenue.

You will likely want to have the items for use as soon as they are ready, so there will be a negative issue of the items fabricated, at the point of completing the job card. This issue will cost to the job card.
